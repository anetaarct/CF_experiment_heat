Distributional sensitivity analysis

For continuous nestling traits, the primary models use Gaussian errors. Student-t models are fitted as robustness checks because AIC can favour heavier-tailed distributions even when the observed trait distributions do not strongly depart from normality.

AIC is therefore reported as descriptive model-comparison information, not as the sole criterion for choosing the biological model. The main interpretation remains based on the Gaussian mixed models; Student-t results are used to check whether treatment-effect directions and support are robust to the distributional assumption.

Interpretation rule

Effects that show the same direction in Gaussian and Student-t models are treated as directionally robust. The asterisk indicates whether the effect also has statistical support (p < 0.05). Effects that change direction, or have support only under one distributional assumption, are interpreted cautiously as distribution-sensitive results.

When the estimated effect changed direction between Gaussian and Student-t models, this indicates that the result was sensitive to distributional assumptions. We therefore do not interpret this effect as robust evidence for a biological relationship.

Dispersion effects

For continuous responses, we also fitted location-scale models in glmmTMB, allowing residual dispersion to differ between experimental treatments. These models ask whether the experiment affected not only the mean response, but also the variability of the trait. Dispersion effects are interpreted cautiously and separately from the primary mean effects.

Positive dispersion estimates indicate higher residual variance relative to the reference group, whereas negative estimates indicate lower residual variance. Treatment-group contrasts

As an additional sensitivity analysis, we also compare the four combined treatment groups (CONCON, CONEXP, EXPCON, and EXPEXP). These contrasts are descriptive and are used to make group-level differences easier to inspect. They do not replace the primary factorial models, which remain the main biological tests of incubation-stage warming, nestling-stage warming, and their interaction.

For nestling traits, the sensitivity model is:

response ~ GROUP + SEX + BS_sc + (1 | F_RING) + (1 | YEAR)

For hatchability, the model is:

HATCHED ~ GROUP + CS_sc + (1 | F_RING) + (1 | YEAR)

The hatchability group comparison is included only as a descriptive sensitivity check, because hatchability occurs before the nestling-stage treatment.
